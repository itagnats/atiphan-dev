# Growing With AI Without Losing Ourselves

Intelligence Jobs — How can AI power opportunity for the next generation?

I used to believe that becoming a better software developer simply meant becoming better at writing code. As a frontend developer, I have watched that definition change every few months. Beyond building interfaces, I am now expected to understand user experience, system design, API integration, backend concepts, DevOps, automated testing, and AI-assisted development. Learning across these disciplines has made me a better engineer. But it has also left me with a question I cannot stop asking.

> What if the hardest part of the AI era is not the technology itself, but the way we expect human beings to adapt to it?

We tell young people that AI will unlock endless opportunity. I believe that is true — but only halfway. AI multiplies opportunity for those who can direct it with judgment, and quietly narrows it for anyone measured only by speed. If we are not careful, AI will not democratize opportunity; it will concentrate it among the fastest adopters while leaving capable, thoughtful people behind. So the question that matters is not whether the next generation can keep up. It is whether we can build a path where AI amplifies what makes each person valuable, instead of pressuring everyone to move at the same machine-like pace.

Right now, many of us feel that pressure as an endless marathon, where pausing to understand something deeply feels like falling behind. Success is increasingly measured by how quickly we adapt rather than how meaningfully we learn. Students are preparing for jobs that do not yet exist; professionals redefine their careers alongside technologies that did not exist last year. This is not a case of one generation being right and another wrong — it is that change has simply accelerated faster than any generation has had to absorb before.

A new kind of trust gap has appeared alongside it. We are encouraged to embrace AI, yet when we learn faster or produce strong work with its help, we are often asked, "Did you really do this yourself?" or "Do you actually understand it?" AI has changed not only how we work but how competence is judged — and we have adopted it far faster than we have agreed on what responsible, AI-assisted expertise even looks like.

There is a deeper mistake hidden in all of this: we have begun expecting people to think like machines. AI processes many tasks at once through parallel computation. Human minds do not. We grow through focus, reflection, repetition, and experience. Yet workplaces increasingly reward constant context-switching and the ability to do everything at once. I have felt how hard it becomes to build real depth when attention is always divided — and how easily that leaves people feeling overwhelmed and disconnected from the craft they once loved.

> This is the belief that now guides me: technology should adapt to humans, not force humans to imitate technology.

That belief changes how I think about AI education. Today most of it centers on AI literacy — writing better prompts, comparing tools, following the workflow some expert shared online. Those skills matter, but they quietly assume there is one "best practice" for everyone. I do not believe there is. AI is not mathematics; there is rarely one correct answer or one universal workflow. Two people using the same model produce entirely different results because they think differently, ask different questions, and solve different problems. The value is not in copying someone else's workflow — it is in understanding your own.

This is the idea I most want to build. I call it Human-Centered AI Adaptation (HCAA): a framework that begins not by teaching people how to use AI, but by helping them understand how they learn, think, and grow alongside it. At its center is what I imagine as a Human-AI Collaboration Compass.

Instead of starting with tutorials, it would start with self-discovery. Through reflective questions drawn from psychology and learning science, a person would explore how they naturally learn, decide, create, and collaborate. Rather than assigning a rigid personality type, the Compass would turn those patterns into a shareable "mind room" — a visual picture of how they currently work with AI and where they could grow. From there the experience becomes hands-on: people experiment rather than just consume advice, learning when AI should lead, when human judgment must stay in control, and which foundations should never be skipped before being accelerated by a tool. Step by step, they build their own way of collaborating with AI — not a copy of someone else's.

The goal is not to produce identical AI users. It is to develop adaptive judgment — the ability to decide, consciously, what to learn deeply, what to accelerate responsibly, and how to work with AI in a way that fits your own strengths and values.

If I am selected as a CP Group One Young World Ambassador, this is exactly where I believe I can contribute most — and where it can matter most for CP. I work within the CP Leadership Institute (CPLI): in effect, the place where the group shapes the thinking of its current and future leaders across every business unit. That makes it the highest-leverage place I can imagine to introduce a human-centered approach to AI. Leaders set direction and culture; when a leader decides how their teams should adopt AI, that decision ripples outward to thousands of people. If CPLI helps leaders adopt AI in a way that amplifies their people rather than pressures them, that single shift in mindset can cascade across retail, agri-food, telecom, and every other part of the group.

Concretely, I would love to help pilot HCAA principles inside a CPLI leadership program — first helping leaders map their own AI-collaboration style with the Compass, then guiding them to think through how they set AI expectations for their teams humanely: where to demand depth, where to encourage acceleration, and how to judge competence fairly in an AI-assisted world. A leader who has examined how they themselves grow alongside AI is far better equipped to build a culture where their people can do the same without losing themselves. And the people who feel this pressure most are the youngest on every team — the new graduates and early-career employees entering CP, whose first years decide whether they thrive or quietly burn out. When their leaders set AI expectations humanely, the next generation inherits opportunity instead of anxiety. That, to me, is how AI powers opportunity at scale — not one employee at a time, but through the people who shape direction for everyone.

From One Young World, I hope to bring back more than inspiration. I want to learn from leaders worldwide who are exploring how technology can advance humanity while respecting cultural diversity, well-being, and human dignity, and to use those perspectives to sharpen HCAA well beyond my own experience. But the next generation should not have to wait for institutions to act. Alongside my work at CP, I plan to keep building the Compass as a free, open project made for young people to use directly. Because each person's "mind room" is designed to be shared, students and early-career creators around the world could post how they collaborate with AI across social media, compare their approaches, and turn what is usually a private struggle into an open, global conversation. Instead of one expert broadcasting the "right" way to use AI, the next generation could shape the framework themselves — peer to peer, across borders — so that opportunity spreads through the very people it is meant for, rather than trickling down to them.

Artificial intelligence will keep reshaping our future. The question is not whether we will adapt, but how — and who gets to benefit when we do. I believe opportunity in the AI era should not belong only to those who learn the fastest or automate the most. It should belong to those who understand themselves deeply enough to decide when to lead, when to let AI lead, and how to keep growing without losing what makes them human.

> Because the future of work is not only about building better AI. It is about building a future where people grow with AI — and remain fully themselves while doing it.
